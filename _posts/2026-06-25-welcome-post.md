Months of diplomatic backchanneling between Washington and Tehran have unravelled this week, as US-Iran ceasefire negotiations collapsed following a sharp dispute over proposed demilitarised zones in the Strait of Hormuz.

<!--more-->

Senior officials on both sides confirmed the breakdown late Tuesday, with each blaming the other for the impasse. The talks, quietly mediated by Oman, were seen as the closest the two adversaries had come to a de-escalation framework since the Gulf crises of 2024.

## What Broke the Deal

The sticking point was Iran's insistence that any ceasefire zone exclude US carrier groups currently operating in the Arabian Sea. Washington refused, calling the demand a non-starter that would fundamentally undermine freedom of navigation.

## Regional Fallout

Gulf Cooperation Council states, which had cautiously endorsed the talks, expressed alarm at the collapse. Saudi and Emirati officials were reportedly given no advance warning, adding to anxieties about regional stability ahead of summer.

## What Comes Next

Analysts expect a return to what one former US diplomat described as "managed tension" — neither full war nor genuine peace. Shipping insurance premiums in the Gulf corridor surged 12% on Wednesday morning as markets digested the news.

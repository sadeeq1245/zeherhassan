Washington said sanctions would cripple China's chip industry. Beijing heard the message differently. Five years after the first major export controls, China has not collapsed — it has adapted, invested, and in some areas, quietly leapfrogged. This is the story of one of the most consequential technology races in human history, told without the hype.

<!--more-->

## The War Nobody Declared

The US-China semiconductor conflict did not begin with a declaration or a treaty. It began with a licensing requirement.

In October 2022, the United States Commerce Department introduced sweeping export controls targeting China's ability to manufacture advanced chips. The restrictions blocked the export of chipmaking equipment capable of producing semiconductors below 14nm, cut off Nvidia's most powerful AI GPUs from Chinese buyers, and invoked the Foreign Direct Product Rule — a legal mechanism that extends US jurisdiction over any product manufactured anywhere in the world using American technology.

Beyond chip exports, the controls also restrict semiconductor manufacturing equipment needed to produce advanced nodes, with particularly tight controls on extreme-ultraviolet lithography machines produced solely by Dutch firm ASML. The restrictions also limit the ability of US persons to support or service advanced semiconductor manufacturing facilities in China, with rules targeting both hardware and knowledge transfer. 

The assumption behind this strategy was clear: deny China the tools, deny China the chips, deny China the future. What happened next was not what Washington expected.

---

## The Numbers Washington Did Not Plan For

ASML, the leading maker of deep ultraviolet lithography systems, sold 70 percent of its DUV systems to Chinese entities in 2024.  In other words, even as EUV machines were blocked, China was stockpiling the previous generation of equipment at an extraordinary rate — building a production base that sanctions had not anticipated.

China's share of the global silicon wafer market sat at roughly 3% in the early 2020s. By 2025, that share had climbed to approximately 28%, and by 2026 it could reach 32%. 

The investment driving this expansion is staggering. In May 2024, China launched its third national semiconductor fund, valued at 344 billion yuan ($47.5 billion), with SMIC positioned as a primary beneficiary to fund its advanced node development and equipment procurement efforts. 

In January 2026, SMIC moved to acquire full control of its Beijing subsidiary, Semiconductor Manufacturing North China, for $5.79 billion — a strategic consolidation designed to tighten control over its northern manufacturing assets. 

This is not a country retreating. It is a country doubling down.

## What China Can Actually Build Right Now

Understanding China's semiconductor position requires separating verified capability from aspirational marketing — because both exist in abundance.

**What is real at 7nm:** In 2023, SMIC officially announced the mass production of 7nm logic chips, marking a milestone that signifies China's international competitiveness in cutting-edge semiconductor manufacturing. Its yield rate reached an impressive 95%, approaching international leading standards. SMIC optimized chip power consumption by innovating with FinFET transistor structures, successfully reducing power usage by 18%. 

**What is contested at 5nm:** Despite reports that Huawei's new PC notebook was powered by a 5nm chip manufactured by SMIC, market research firm TechInsights confirmed it was manufactured using SMIC's 7nm process node. SMIC has not yet achieved a 5nm equivalent node that can be produced at scale.  The company can produce 5nm chips at low quantities but yields remain poor.

SMIC is successfully advancing its process technology to near-leading-edge nodes by refining older DUV lithography, an approach that demonstrates significant engineering capability but comes at the cost of poor yields and high production expenses. 

The honest picture: China is firmly at 7nm and inching toward 5nm — while TSMC and Samsung have already moved to 2nm production.

---

## The Huawei Factor: A Company Sanctions Built

No entity better illustrates the paradox of the semiconductor war than Huawei. Blacklisted, cut off from Google services, denied access to TSMC, and subjected to successive waves of US restrictions — Huawei responded by becoming a semiconductor powerhouse.

In 2026, Huawei not only builds competitive system-on-chips for smartphones and PCs as well as 5G modems, but it also offers competitive AI accelerators that can challenge some of Nvidia's data center GPUs. The company also builds rack-scale AI solutions that can challenge Nvidia's NVL72 GB200. 

Huawei's AI chip revenue is projected to surge 60% year-on-year to $12 billion in 2026. 

SMIC's most critical partner is Huawei, for whom it serves as the exclusive foundry for the Ascend series of AI chips. It is also building alliances with Alibaba Group to develop and manufacture custom 5nm AI chips for data centre needs. 

Huawei has emerged as the most credible challenger to Nvidia within the Chinese market. Its Ascend 910C AI accelerator has gained significant traction among Chinese enterprises, and Huawei is targeting 1.6 million Ascend dies across its product line by 2026. The company has also built its own AI computing framework — MindSpore — as an open-source alternative to Nvidia's CUDA software ecosystem. 

The deeper irony: Huawei's exclusion from the global supply chain forced it to invest in capabilities it would never have built otherwise. Washington created the conditions for the competitor it feared.

## The Secret Lab in Shenzhen: China's EUV Machine

The most consequential and least discussed development in China's semiconductor race is happening in a high-security laboratory.

In a high-security Shenzhen laboratory, Chinese scientists have built what Washington has spent years trying to prevent: a prototype of a machine capable of producing cutting-edge semiconductor chips. Completed in early 2025 and now undergoing testing, the prototype fills nearly an entire factory floor. It was built by a team of former engineers from Dutch semiconductor giant ASML. 

This is China's domestic EUV machine — the holy grail of the semiconductor war.

Multiple intelligence assessments from the US, Dutch, and South Korean governments reported in 2024-2025 that China had achieved EUV-range wavelength generation in laboratory conditions but had not demonstrated an integrated, production-capable EUV tool. The gap between laboratory demonstration and production-capable machine remains significant. 

A prototype domestic EUV machine is in testing, with functional chips targeted by 2028 and a 2030 timeline considered more realistic by outside analysts. 

If China achieves a working, production-capable EUV machine — even one significantly less efficient than ASML's systems — it would be the single most consequential technology event of the decade. Every export control Washington has constructed is built on the assumption that China cannot build this machine. The Shenzhen laboratory is a direct challenge to that assumption.

## The Equipment Race: China's Companies Go Global

Localization of equipment and materials is gaining traction. YMTC has established a pilot line using domestic wafer fabrication equipment, and SMIC's FAB3 is building its baseline process using domestically sourced tools. 

Three Chinese chip equipment manufacturers ranked among the world's top 20 by sales volume for the first time in 2025. ACM Research, AMEC, and Naura Technology Group are generating significant revenue and scale. NAURA has entered the top tier of global semiconductor equipment vendors, ranked eighth globally in 2024. 

In parallel with chip development, China has implemented a "50% Mandate" requiring domestic fabrication plants to source at least half of their manufacturing equipment from local vendors. ASML has already warned investors that its 2026 China revenue will decline significantly as a result. 

---

## Washington Escalates: The MATCH Act

As China advances, Washington is tightening the noose further.

Previous rounds of US export controls targeted ASML's most advanced technology: EUV lithography machines. The Dutch company has never shipped an EUV machine to China, and the Netherlands has cooperated with restrictions since 2023. The MATCH Act closes the remaining loophole: it would ban the export of DUV immersion lithography systems — the older-generation machines that Chinese chipmakers have been using to manufacture less advanced semiconductors. 

The commercial fallout for ASML is already visible. China accounted for roughly one-third of ASML's sales in 2025. That number plunged to 19% during the first quarter of 2026. ASML has already warned investors that its 2026 China revenue will decline significantly. 

But there is a counterargument that deserves serious consideration. The irony is that aggressive export controls are accelerating China's domestic semiconductor investment rather than preventing it. Each escalation has pushed Chinese developers and fabs deeper into the domestic ecosystem. 

Last year, SMIC and other Chinese firms bought $38 billion in equipment from the five top allied semiconductor manufacturing equipment suppliers — an increase of 66% from 2022, when the United States introduced country-wide export controls on advanced chipmaking tools.  Sanctions, paradoxically, drove a buying surge before the doors closed.

---

## The 2030 Target: What Beijing Is Actually Aiming For

China's 15th Five-Year Plan, covering 2026 to 2030, targets five key priorities: advanced logic process nodes pushing 7nm/5nm production and yield optimisation; memory industry expansion including scaling YMTC and CXMT output and initiating HBM development; breakthroughs in lithography; localisation of core equipment, subsystems, and materials; and advances in EDA tools enabling a self-reliant design software ecosystem. 

China's new semiconductor plan has set an 80% self-sufficiency target and a 2030 goal of building and validating a 7nm production line using entirely domestic equipment. 

The key distinction is what "self-sufficiency" actually means in practice. China does not need to out-innovate TSMC. It needs to produce AI chips good enough for domestic commercial AI development, manufactured domestically, without dependence on US technology. On that more modest but strategically critical standard, China's progress through 2026 is significant. 

---

## The Honest Scorecard

After hundreds of billions spent and years of escalating restrictions, here is where the chip war actually stands:

China has built dozens of fabs across the country producing chips on a variety of production nodes. YMTC has developed world-class 3D NAND memory with its Xtacking architecture. ChangXin Memory Technologies established domestic DRAM production at mainstream nodes, successfully ramped volume manufacturing, and quickly established positions in local markets. 

SMIC's founder Richard Chang argues that advanced nodes account for less than 20% of the global semiconductor market by product volume, while more than 80% of demand comes from mature-node and specialty-process segments. Many niche markets still dominated by overseas players represent key breakthrough opportunities for Chinese semiconductor companies. 

But the gap at the frontier remains large. Estimates suggest China will be able to produce advanced chips at roughly 1-4% of US production capacity in 2025, declining to 1-2% in 2026 as US and allied chipmakers continue to scale.  At the absolute cutting edge — sub-3nm chips — China remains years behind and structurally unable to close that gap without a domestic EUV breakthrough.

---

## My View

This is one of those stories where both sides of the mainstream debate are partially wrong.

The hawks in Washington who argue that export controls have failed are not reading the data correctly. China is years behind at the frontier, and without access to EUV, that gap is not narrowing at the leading edge. TSMC's 2nm chips are not something China can replicate in this decade through DUV multi-patterning tricks.

But the optimists who say Washington has the situation under control are also misreading the field. China does not need to match TSMC to win strategically. It needs to produce chips good enough to power its own AI ecosystem — domestically, reliably, without a killswitch Washington controls. At that more achievable threshold, China is closer than most Western analysts admit.

The Shenzhen EUV prototype is the number that matters most and receives the least attention. If that machine achieves production viability — even in 2029, even at half the efficiency of ASML's best tools — every export control Washington has spent a decade constructing is undermined. That is the bet Beijing is making. It is not an irrational one.

What is being built in those Chinese clean rooms right now is not just a chip. It is the infrastructure for a parallel technological civilisation — one that runs on different software, different standards, different supply chains, and different rules. The semiconductor war is the physical manifestation of a deeper bifurcation.

That bifurcation is not coming. It is already here.
